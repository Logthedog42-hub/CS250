1. Interpreting User Needs & User Stories
Interpreting user needs isn't about guessing what a client wants based on a massive, static document; it's about breaking down their core goals into functional, bite-sized requirements. Creating user stories changes the entire process by shifting the focus from rigid technical specifications to the actual human value of a feature. Writing stories in the standard format keeps the development team grounded in why a piece of code matters to a real person. On the SNHU Travel project, this approach stopped us from building bloated, useless features and allowed us to establish a clear definition of "done" for every single task, ensuring what we shipped actually aligned with customer expectations.

2. Program Development Approach & Future Agile Processes
My approach to building software focuses on continuous, incremental progress rather than trying to build a massive application all at once. Working in short, time-boxed sprints allows for rapid development, early testing, and immediate feedback loops. Moving forward into future development work, I absolutely plan to carry over the practice of daily standups and maintaining a highly visible digital board like Jira. Having a single source of truth for the project's state prevents duplicate work and makes roadblocks obvious before they stall progress. Additionally, integrating testing simultaneously with coding is a practice I hope to maintain, as catching bugs in a matter of minutes saves weeks of painful debugging at a final deadline.

3. Being a Good Team Member in Software Development
In software development, being a good team member means prioritizing transparency, adaptability, and active collaboration over working in an isolated vacuum. It means communicating status changes immediately, especially when things go wrong, so the team can pivot together. When our team faced an unexpected requirement change with the wellness slideshow feature, success didn't come from individuals panicking or hoarding code; it came from open communication and a shared willingness to adjust the plan during our next planning cycle. Ultimately, a great developer doesn't just write clean code; they shield their peers from unnecessary complexity, respect the roles of the Product Owner and Scrum Master, and actively contribute to a cohesive, blame-free engineering culture.
